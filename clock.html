Digital Clock PCB

2023

PCB Design

A clock is a highly utilized yet surprisingly simple digital circuit. In this project, I wanted to implement a clock at a low abstraction level and then transfer this design into a custom PCB. This project would give me the opportunity to explore KiCAD PCB design as well as low abstraction (non-microprocessor) digital circuit design. The main priority of this project design was to create a fully functional 24 hour digital clock with the least amount of total expenditure.

Design Process

The initial design decision involved choosing between a Nixie tube clock or a traditional 7 segment display clock. I found that the Nixie tube design looked much better however this came at the cost of reliability, circuit complexity and circuit cost. Ultimately given that PCB cost was correlated to the surface area of the board,which in turn is correlated to circuit complexity, I decided to go with a 7 segment display clock design.

Given this decision, the next step was to create a synchronous system clock circuit. This circuit would be responsible for the “timekeeping” function of the 24 hour clock. For clock design, I considered both a 555 timer IC circuit as well as a crystal oscillator circuit both functioning at a constant frequency of 60 Hz. Ultimately I chose to go with a crystal oscillator circuit. A 555 timer had the advantage of circuit simplicity due to the fact that the circuit only consisted of 1 IC and a few simple circuit elements. This provided the advantage of circuit space: a major advantage in PCB design. However, this simplicity came at the disadvantage of clock accuracy. Since the 555 IC relies on resistors and capacitors to maintain a constant clock, any variations in temperature and external conditions would result in deviations of the clock. The crystal oscillator on the other hand had the benefit of high clock accuracy at the disadvantage of circuit complexity. This complexity stems from the fact that a crystal oscillator of 60 hz does not exist commercially. Instead, a larger frequency crystal must be used and the corresponding signal must go through a frequency divider circuit in order to get our desired frequency signal. Therefore, this circuit would have at minimum 2 ICs and a crystal oscillator.

Ultimately, I decided that the clock accuracy of the crystal oscillator was worth the additional space and complexity. All that is remaining on this project is to create the PCB design in KiCad.I plan on completing and producing this PCB within the upcoming weeks.
